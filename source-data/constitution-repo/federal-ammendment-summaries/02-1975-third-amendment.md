Before this amendment, the 1973 Constitution provided relatively tight protections against arbitrary arrest; the state had to work quickly to justify holding someone. This change effectively tilted the scales in favor of the government’s security apparatus. By tripling the time someone could be held before a review, the state gained a much longer window to detain political opponents or activists without the immediate interference of the judiciary.

For the average citizen, this meant that the constitutional "shield" against being picked up and held in secret was weakened. It gave the executive branch more breathing room to keep people in custody during times of political unrest. Additionally, by making it harder to end a state of emergency, the government ensured that special, more restrictive powers could stay in place for longer periods with less frequent parliamentary interference.
