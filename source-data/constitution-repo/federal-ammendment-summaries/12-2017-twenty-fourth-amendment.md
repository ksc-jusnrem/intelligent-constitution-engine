In 2017, Pakistan was facing a constitutional "math problem" with a very tight deadline. A new national census had been conducted earlier that year (the first in 19 years), but the official, final results were taking a long time to be formally published. Under the existing rules, the government could only redraw election boundaries based on final published census data.

With the 2018 General Elections fast approaching, the political atmosphere was anxious. If the government waited for the final report, there wouldn't be enough time to adjust the number of seats for each district, potentially delaying the elections or making them legally "unfair" because they would be based on 20-year-old population data. This amendment was a one-time emergency fix to allow the upcoming elections to proceed using the provisional (preliminary) census results instead.

The 24th Amendment was essentially a "legal bridge" that prevented a massive constitutional deadlock. Without it, the 2018 elections would have been held using population data from 1998, which would have been highly inaccurate. By allowing the use of preliminary 2017 data, the law ensured that the number of representatives each province sent to Islamabad actually matched the number of people living there today.

For the average citizen, the "Then vs. Now" impact was about fair representation. It meant that if your city’s population had doubled since the 90s, you were finally going to get the appropriate number of representatives in Parliament. It protected the citizen's right to a "timely election" by removing a bureaucratic hurdle that could have postponed the entire democratic process. It was a rare moment where all political parties agreed to a shortcut to ensure the 2018 vote could happen on time and with updated maps.
