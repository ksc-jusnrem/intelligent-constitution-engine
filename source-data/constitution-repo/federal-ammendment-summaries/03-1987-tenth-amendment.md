By 1987, Pakistan was operating under the semi-civilian setup established after the 1985 non-party elections. While the military’s direct Martial Law had ended, the government—led by Prime Minister Muhammad Khan Junejo under the watchful eye of President Zia-ul-Haq—was looking for ways to streamline government operations. The original 1973 Constitution had set very high requirements for how often Parliament must meet, which was becoming difficult to maintain in a political environment where the executive held most of the decision-making power. The Tenth Amendment was a "housekeeping" move to reduce the mandatory workload of the legislature.

The "then vs. now" of this amendment is a story of quantity versus oversight. In the original 1973 vision, the high number of mandatory working days (160) was designed to ensure that the Parliament was a permanent, bustling center of power. It forced the government to constantly face the opposition and answer questions for the majority of the year.

By reducing these days, the Tenth Amendment effectively made the Parliament a "part-time" body. For the average citizen, this meant their elected representatives were spending less time debating laws and scrutinizing the government's budget and actions. While it made the government’s life easier by reducing the time they had to spend defending their policies on the floor of the House, it ultimately weakened the Parliament's role as a 24/7 watchdog over the country’s affairs.
