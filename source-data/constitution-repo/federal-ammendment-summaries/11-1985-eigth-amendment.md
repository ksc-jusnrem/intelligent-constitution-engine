After General Zia-ul-Haq overthrew the government in 1977, Pakistan lived under Martial Law for eight years. In 1985, Zia wanted to transition to a civilian government while ensuring he retained ultimate control. He held non-party elections and handpicked Muhammad Khan Junejo as Prime Minister. However, before Zia would agree to lift Martial Law and restore the Constitution, he demanded this amendment. It was the "price" the newly elected Parliament had to pay to get the military to formally step back from daily rule.

The Eighth Amendment turned the President from a "ceremonial figurehead" into the most powerful person in Pakistan. In a healthy parliamentary democracy, the Prime Minister is the captain of the ship. After this amendment, the President became the owner of the ship who could fire the captain and sink the entire boat (the Parliament) whenever he felt it was necessary.

For the average citizen, this meant a period of extreme political instability. Because the President had the power to dissolve assemblies, Pakistan saw four different governments dismissed between 1985 and 1997. It created a "Sword of Damocles" hanging over every elected government; if a Prime Minister disagreed with the President or the military establishment, the President could simply use Article 58(2)(b) to send everyone home and call for new elections. This effectively shifted the "final word" in Pakistani politics from the voters and their representatives to a single, non-elected office.
