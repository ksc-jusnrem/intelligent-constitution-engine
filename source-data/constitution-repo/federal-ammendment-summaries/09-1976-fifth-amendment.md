By 1976, the government of Zulfikar Ali Bhutto was increasingly at odds with the superior judiciary. The courts were frequently issuing "stay orders" and granting bail to political detainees, which the administration viewed as an obstacle to executive governance and national security.

The Fifth Amendment represented a significant shift in the Separation of Powers, moving Pakistan toward a system where the executive branch held the upper hand over the judiciary. Before this amendment, the Chief Justice held their position until retirement, providing a level of stability and independence from political whims. By introducing term limits and the power to transfer judges without consent, the government gained the ability to "rotate out" or "relocate" judges who were perceived as being too critical of the state.

For the ordinary citizen, the most direct impact was the loss of a legal safety net. If a citizen was arrested or detained by the government, the High Court’s power to intervene via bail was effectively stripped away in many scenarios. This meant that the state could keep individuals in custody for longer periods without judicial oversight, significantly weakening the constitutional protections and individual rights that were originally intended to balance the state's power.
