By late 1976, Prime Minister Zulfikar Ali Bhutto was preparing for upcoming general elections and wanted to ensure stability within the judicial branch—or, more accurately, to retain "favorable" leadership in the courts. The previous amendment (the Fifth) had already introduced a five-year term for the Chief Justice. However, a specific problem arose: the sitting Chief Justice of the Supreme Court, Justice Yaqub Ali, was set to hit the mandatory retirement age of 65 before his five-year term was up. The Sixth Amendment was widely seen as a "tailor-made" law designed to keep him in power longer.

The Sixth Amendment finalized the government’s grip on the judiciary by creating what critics call "person-specific" law. Before this, the retirement age was a hard line in the sand; once a judge turned 65, they had to go, making way for the next senior judge. By allowing a Chief Justice to stay past that age to finish a "term," the government effectively broke the natural line of succession. This prevented more independent or "unfavorable" senior judges from ascending to the top spot.

For the average citizen, this meant the highest court in the land was no longer functioning solely on the principle of seniority and merit, but rather on rules that could be bent to keep specific individuals in office. It turned the position of Chief Justice into something that felt more like a political appointment than a neutral judicial office. This further eroded the "legal safety net" we discussed, as the government proved it could change the Constitution simply to keep its preferred "referee" on the field.
