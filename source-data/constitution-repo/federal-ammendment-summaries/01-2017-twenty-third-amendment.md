The 23rd Amendment was essentially a "Part 2" to the 21st Amendment. When the military courts were established in 2015 to handle terrorism cases after the Peshawar school attack, they were given a strict two-year "sunset clause." By January 2017, that time had run out, and the military courts technically ceased to exist.

However, the government and military argued that the threat of terrorism was still too high and the civilian courts were still too vulnerable to handle these specific high-profile cases. The political atmosphere was tense; while some lawmakers were wary of extending military powers over civilians, the consensus was that the "job wasn't finished." This amendment was sought to bring back the military courts for another two-year period.

The 23rd Amendment was a "renewal of an emergency lease" on power. In simple terms, it told the country that the normal rules of justice still weren't enough to handle the security crisis. By passing this, the Parliament admitted that the civilian court system hadn't been reformed enough in the previous two years to take these cases back. It kept the "fast-track" military trial system alive, ensuring that suspects could be tried and sentenced quickly without the long delays typical of the standard legal process.

For the citizen, this meant that for another two years, the military remained a central player in the country's legal and judicial landscape. While it was marketed as a necessary tool to fight "the worst of the worst," it highlighted a "Then vs. Now" reality where the exception (military trials for civilians) was starting to look like a recurring habit rather than a one-time emergency. It reinforced a system where, for a specific group of people, the constitutional right to a trial in a traditional civilian court was suspended in favor of national security.
