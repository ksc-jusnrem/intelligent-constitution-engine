The 21st Amendment was a direct response to one of the darkest days in Pakistan's history: the terror attack on the Army Public School (APS) in Peshawar on December 16, 2014. The brutal massacre of schoolchildren created an intense national outcry for immediate and severe justice.

The political and military leadership felt that the civilian court system was too slow and that witnesses or judges were often too intimidated to convict hardened terrorists. In an atmosphere of "national emergency," this amendment was sought to bypass the traditional legal system for specific terror-related cases to ensure swift, high-speed trials.

The 21st Amendment created a "fast-track" lane for justice that operated outside the normal civilian rules. Before this, any person accused of a crime—no matter how heinous—had to be tried in a civilian court with standard rights of defense and public hearings. After this amendment, the military was given the power to act as judge and jury for terrorism suspects, leading to much faster convictions and executions.

For the average citizen, this represented a significant trade-off: the country sacrificed a degree of "due process" and judicial independence in exchange for the promise of national security and "speedy justice." It shifted the balance of power heavily toward the military establishment, allowing them to handle internal threats with the same legal authority usually reserved for a battlefield. While it was intended only for "terrorists using the name of religion," it sparked intense debate about whether it set a dangerous precedent for bypassing the fundamental rights of a citizen in the future.
