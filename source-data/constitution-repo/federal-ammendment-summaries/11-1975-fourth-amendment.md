The "then vs. now" shift of this amendment was largely about weakening the citizen's legal shield. Before this change, if a person was arrested for political reasons, they could appeal to a High Court for immediate bail. After this amendment, the High Courts essentially had their "hands tied"—they were legally forbidden from releasing anyone held under detention laws. This shifted a massive amount of power from the judges to the government's executive branch.

Furthermore, the "60-day rule" for court orders meant that the government could effectively outwait the legal system. If the government took an action (like collecting a controversial tax or seizing property) and a court paused it, the government only had to wait two months for that pause to disappear. This made it much harder for ordinary citizens or businesses to use the courts to stop government overreach in a meaningful way.
