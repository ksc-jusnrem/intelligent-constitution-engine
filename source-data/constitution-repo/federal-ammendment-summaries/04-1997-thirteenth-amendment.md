After a decade of extreme political instability, the 1997 elections gave Nawaz Sharif a "heavy mandate" (a massive two-thirds majority) in Parliament. Since 1985, Pakistan had seen four different governments dismissed by various Presidents using the powers given to them by General Zia’s 8th Amendment. The political atmosphere was one of deep frustration with the "musical chairs" of democracy. Sharif used his overwhelming majority to strike back at the presidency and reclaim the authority that had been stripped from the Prime Minister’s office over the previous twelve years.

The 13th Amendment was the "Great Restoration" for the Prime Minister. If the 8th Amendment had turned the President into a king-like figure who could end a government on a whim, the 13th Amendment turned the President back into a ceremonial figurehead. The "then vs. now" shift was dramatic: before this, the Prime Minister lived in constant fear of being dismissed by the President; after this, the Prime Minister became the undisputed "captain of the ship" once again.

For the average citizen, this meant the end of the "Sword of Damocles" hanging over their elected representatives. It was intended to bring stability to the country by ensuring that only the voters (through their representatives) could decide when a government should end, not a single individual in the President's House. However, by removing the "safety valve" that allowed the President to resolve political deadlocks, it also meant that if a Prime Minister became too powerful or faced a crisis, there was no longer a constitutional way to remove them other than a vote of no-confidence or, as history eventually showed, a military coup.
