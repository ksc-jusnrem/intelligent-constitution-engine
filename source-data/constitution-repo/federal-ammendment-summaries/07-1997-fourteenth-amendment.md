During the 1990s, Pakistani politics was plagued by "lotas"—a term used for politicians who switched loyalties for money or power (horse-trading). This constant "floor-crossing" made it impossible for any government to stay stable, as members of Parliament would often abandon their party to join the opposition during a crucial vote. After Nawaz Sharif won a massive majority in 1997, he was determined to end this practice. The 14th Amendment was his tool to "lock" members into their party lines and prevent the internal rebellions that had collapsed previous governments.

The 14th Amendment shifted the balance of power from the individual lawmaker to the Party Leader. Before this, an MP could vote according to their conscience (or a bribe) without necessarily losing their job. After this amendment, the Party Head became a virtual dictator over their members. If an MP disagreed with a single party policy or chose not to show up for a vote, the Party Head could effectively "fire" them from Parliament.

For the average citizen, this was a double-edged sword. On one hand, it brought a much-needed end to the "musical chairs" of government and stabilized the democratic system against bribery. On the other hand, it turned elected representatives into "voting machines" for their leaders. The "then vs. now" reality meant that your local representative could no longer represent your specific interests if those interests clashed with the national party’s agenda—doing so would mean political suicide. It silenced internal dissent and made the leadership of political parties almost untouchable.
