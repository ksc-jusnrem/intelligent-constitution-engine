By 2016, Pakistan was preparing for future general elections while still dealing with the fallout and accusations of "rigging" from previous polls. A major point of contention was the eligibility criteria for the Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP). Historically, only retired judges could lead the Commission.

There was a growing political consensus that the ECP needed more than just legal expertise; it needed administrative experience and "technocratic" knowledge to handle the massive logistical challenge of a modern election. The 22nd Amendment was introduced to diversify the leadership of the ECP and ensure that the Commission remained functional even when political leaders couldn't agree on appointments.

The 22nd Amendment was about professionalizing the "referees" of Pakistan’s democracy. Before this, the ECP was seen almost exclusively as a legal body run by retired judges. By opening the doors to retired bureaucrats and experts (technocrats), the amendment acknowledged that running an election is as much a massive management and IT task as it is a legal one.

For the average citizen, the "then vs. now" is significant. Previously, if the top politicians disagreed on appointments, the system could stall. Now, there are strict deadlines and "staggered" terms to ensure that the Election Commission is always staffed and ready to work. Furthermore, by giving the ECP control over local government elections, it brought more transparency to the "third tier" of democracy—the local counselors and mayors who impact a citizen's daily life the most. Essentially, it made the election machinery more robust, permanent, and less dependent on the whims of a few top leaders.
