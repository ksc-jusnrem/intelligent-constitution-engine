In the early 1990s, the government of Prime Minister Nawaz Sharif faced a severe wave of violent crime, sectarian killings, and "sensational" murders. The public was terrified, and the traditional judicial system was seen as too slow and bogged down by procedure to provide a deterrent. The government believed that "justice delayed is justice denied," and sought this amendment to bypass the regular courts and create a parallel system that could hand out punishments rapidly to restore public order.

Before this, every citizen had the right to a trial in a standard court where established rules of evidence and lengthy appeal processes served as a safety net against wrongful conviction. This amendment prioritized speed over those traditional protections, effectively creating a "shadow" judiciary controlled largely by the Federal Government.

For the average citizen, this meant that if they were accused of a crime the government labeled as "heinous," they could be tried, convicted, and have their appeal exhausted all within 60 days. While the goal was to scare criminals and provide "speedy justice," it significantly shifted the balance of power toward the executive. By cutting out the regular High Courts' oversight, the amendment weakened the long-standing constitutional right to a fair and deliberate trial, trading deep judicial scrutiny for a quick verdict.
