The amendment was introduced during a period of intense political polarization and followed a short-lived military conflict with India in May 2025. The government, led by the Shehbaz Sharif coalition, argued that the changes were necessary to modernize national security and fix a massive backlog of over 50,000 cases in the Supreme Court. However, critics and legal experts viewed the speed of the bill’s passage—introduced and passed within days—as a move to consolidate power and shield the executive and military leadership from future legal challenges.

Key Changes :
- Establishment of the Federal Constitutional Court (FCC): A new top-tier court was created specifically to handle constitutional matters. Previously, the Supreme Court of Pakistan held this authority; now, the Supreme Court is largely relegated to an appellate court for non-constitutional civil and criminal cases.
- Military Command Restructuring: The amendment created the post of Chief of Defence Forces (CDF), a position held by the Army Chief (currently Field Marshal Asim Munir), granting him primacy over the Navy and Air Force.
- Lifetime Immunity: Article 248 was amended to grant the President lifetime immunity from criminal prosecution and arrest. Similar protections and lifetime status (rank and uniform) were extended to five-star military officers.
- Judicial Appointments & Transfers: The Executive now has a significant role in appointing the first batch of FCC judges. Furthermore, the Judicial Commission was empowered to transfer High Court judges between provinces; if a judge refuses a transfer, they face disciplinary action.


In simple terms, the 27th Amendment fundamentally changed the "rules of the game" for how the government, the courts, and the military interact. Before this, the Supreme Court was the final word on what the Constitution meant. Now, that power sits with the new Federal Constitutional Court, whose initial judges were picked with heavy influence from the sitting Prime Minister and President. This makes it much harder for the judiciary to act as a "check" on government decisions.

For the average citizen, the most visible change is the "shield" placed around top leaders. By granting lifetime immunity to the President and top generals, the amendment ensures they cannot be held legally accountable in a court of law for their actions even after they leave office. Additionally, the centralization of military power under the new "Chief of Defence Forces" formalizes the military’s role at the heart of Pakistan's governance, moving the country further toward a hybrid system where civilian and military authorities are deeply entwined.
