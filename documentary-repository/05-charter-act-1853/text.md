# Charter Act 1853
## An Act to Provide for the Government of India
### 16 & 17 Victoria, Chapter 95 | Royal Assent: 20 August 1853

---

## Preamble

WHEREAS it is expedient to continue the powers granted by Parliament to the East India Company, and to make further provision for the government of the British territories in India; and whereas it is expedient to separate in a more complete manner the legislative from the executive functions of the Governor-General in Council, and to enlarge the legislative body so as to include additional members more representative of the several provinces and interests of India:

---

## Part I — Continuation of the Charter (Open Term)

**Section 1.** The powers granted to the East India Company for the government and management of the affairs of the said Company, and of the British territories in India, are hereby continued and renewed; and the Company shall continue to possess and manage the said territories and revenues, and to hold and exercise the rights, powers, and privileges previously vested in it, until Parliament shall otherwise direct.

*[Note: Unlike all previous Charters, no terminal date was fixed. Parliament reserved the right to resume direct control at any time. The Mutiny of 1857 caused Parliament to exercise that right within five years.]*

---

## Part II — The Indian Civil Service and Competitive Examinations

**Section 32.** Such appointments to the Civil Service of the East India Company as are now made by the Directors of the said Company shall hereafter be made by open competition; and such appointments shall be made without reference to the fact of any candidate being or not being nominated by a Director.

**Section 33.** His Majesty may from time to time, on the recommendation of the Board of Control, make regulations for the examination of persons desirous of entering the Civil Service of the Company; and all appointments to the said Service shall be made from among those who shall have passed such examinations successfully.

**Section 34.** No person shall be prohibited from being a candidate for appointment to the Civil Service of the said Company by reason only of his being a native of the East Indies or otherwise.

---

## Part III — Separation of Legislative and Executive Councils

**Section 22.** There shall be associated with the Governor-General of India in the exercise of the legislative functions of his Council, six additional members, who shall be called Legislative Councillors; of whom one shall be appointed by the Lieutenant-Governor of Bengal, one by the Lieutenant-Governor of the North-West Provinces, one by the Governor of Bombay, one by the Governor of Madras, one by the Lieutenant-Governor of the Punjab, and one shall be a legal member to be appointed by the Governor-General.

**Section 23.** The Governor-General in Legislative Council — being the Governor-General with his Executive Council and the six Legislative Councillors — shall have the sole power of making laws and regulations for persons and places subject to the government of the Company. The Executive Council alone, without the Legislative Councillors, shall exercise all executive, financial, and military functions.

**Section 24.** Any Legislative Councillor may introduce a Bill; but no Bill involving the raising or appropriation of revenue shall be introduced without the consent of the Governor-General.

---

## Part IV — Parliamentary Committee on Indian Affairs

**Section 55.** There shall be appointed a Standing Committee of the House of Commons for the consideration of Indian affairs; and all despatches to and from the Court of Directors, and all regulations made in India, shall be laid before the said Committee, and may be inquired into by it.

---

## Historical Note

The Charter Act 1853 is the constitutional document that most clearly shows the trajectory away from Company rule. By opening the Civil Service to competition, it implicitly acknowledged that the Company's claim to govern India through patronage was an anachronism. By separating the Legislative from the Executive Council, it planted the seed of legislative representation that would grow into the elected councils of 1861, 1892, 1909, 1919, and 1935.

The Mutiny of 1857 — triggered by the issue of greased cartridges but fuelled by decades of dispossession, cultural disruption, and the absence of any legitimate political channel — made the open-ended charter irrelevant. Parliament passed the Government of India Act 1858 in less than a year.
