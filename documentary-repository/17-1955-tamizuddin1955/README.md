# Federation of Pakistan v. Maulvi Tamizuddin Khan

**Era:** Dominion of Pakistan — the Interim Framework (1947–1956)  
**Date:** 1955-03-21  
**Type:** case

## Dossier

The Assembly's President sued and won before the Sindh Chief Court, which held the dissolution unlawful. On appeal the Federal Court, per Chief Justice Muhammad Munir, reversed — holding (4–1, Cornelius J. dissenting) that laws of the Constituent Assembly required the Governor-General's assent, so the very statute conferring the court's writ jurisdiction was invalid.

## Key provisions and effects

- Dissolution left standing on a technicality of assent
- Cornelius J.'s dissent became the celebrated counter-tradition of Pakistani constitutionalism
- Followed by Usif Patel (1955), which blocked the Governor-General from filling the void by decree
- Governor-General's Reference (1955): Munir court invoked the doctrine of state necessity — 'that which is otherwise not lawful, necessity makes lawful' — to bridge the gap and order a new Assembly

## How it ended

The necessity doctrine seeded here was invoked for every later extra-constitutional intervention, until curtailed in Asma Jilani (1972).
