# Charter Act 1833 (Government of India Act 1833)
## An Act for Effecting an Arrangement with the East India Company, and for the Better Government of His Majesty's Indian Territories
### 3 & 4 William IV, Chapter 85 | Royal Assent: 28 August 1833

---

## Preamble

WHEREAS it is expedient to make further provisions for the government of the East India Company's affairs in India, to define and limit the Company's functions, and to provide for the codification of the laws governing the said territories; and whereas it is just and expedient to declare that no native of the said territories, nor any natural-born subject of His Majesty resident therein, shall, by reason only of his religion, place of birth, descent, or colour, be disabled from holding any place, office, or employment under the said Company:

---

## Part I — Cessation of Commercial Functions

**Section 1.** From and after the twenty-second day of April in the year one thousand eight hundred and thirty-four, the trade between the United Kingdom and the East Indies, and the trade between India and China, shall be laid open; and the exclusive privileges of the Company with respect thereto are hereby declared to have ceased and determined.

**Section 2.** The United Company of Merchants of England trading to the East Indies shall cease to carry on any mercantile or commercial business whatsoever; and all stocks, warehouses, merchandise, and commercial assets shall be applied to the liquidation of the Company's debts, and thereafter shall be transferred to the Crown.

---

## Part II — Governor-General of India

**Section 40.** The Governor-General of the Presidency of Fort William in Bengal shall be styled and known as the Governor-General of India; and he shall have the superintendence, direction, and control of the whole civil, military, and revenue government of all the territories and revenues of the East India Company in India.

**Section 41.** The Governors of the Presidencies of Fort St. George and Bombay, and the Lieutenant-Governors of other territories, shall be subordinate to the Governor-General of India in all matters of civil, military, and revenue administration.

**Section 42.** The Governor-General of India shall have full power and authority to superintend, direct, and control the government of all the said territories, and to make all such regulations as shall seem to him necessary for the administration of justice, the promotion of trade, and the general welfare of the inhabitants of the said territories.

---

## Part III — The Legislative Council

**Section 44.** There shall be a Legislative Council for India, to consist of the Governor-General and his ordinary Council, together with an additional member appointed by the Crown, who shall be a barrister of not less than five years' standing, to be called the Law Member of Council.

**Section 45.** The Governor-General in Legislative Council shall have power to make laws and regulations for all persons and in all places subject to the government of the Company in India, whether British-born or others, whether Europeans or Indians.

---

## Part IV — The Indian Law Commission

**Section 53.** It shall be lawful for the Governor-General in Council to appoint a Commission of not more than five Commissioners, to be called the Indian Law Commission, for the purpose of inquiring into the jurisdiction, powers, and rules of the several Courts of Justice in the Company's territories in India, and into all the laws and customs having the force of law to which the inhabitants thereof are subject; and for the purpose of reporting how far it may be advisable to assimilate the laws and usages applicable to different classes of inhabitants, and what alterations should be made in the laws of India with a view to their amendment, consolidation, and codification.

*[Note: Thomas Babington Macaulay was appointed the first Law Member of Council in 1834. The Commission produced: the Indian Penal Code (1860), the Code of Criminal Procedure (1861), and the Evidence Act (1872) — the statutory skeleton of Pakistani criminal law to this day.]*

---

## Part V — The Non-Discrimination Clause

**Section 87.** No native of the said territories, nor any natural-born subject of His Majesty resident therein, shall, by reason only of his religion, place of birth, descent, colour, or any of them, be disabled from holding any place, office, or employment under the said Company.

---

## Historical Note

Section 87 is the first non-discrimination clause in the constitutional history of the subcontinent. Though honoured far more in the breach than in the observance — the Indian Civil Service remained overwhelmingly European until competitive examinations were partially extended in 1853 — its principle entered the constitutional tradition and was echoed in Article 25 of the Constitution of Pakistan 1973.

The Indian Law Commission's work was foundational. Macaulay's Indian Penal Code (enacted 1860, drafted 1837) remains in force in Pakistan as the Pakistan Penal Code; the Code of Criminal Procedure and the Evidence Act (Qanun-e-Shahadat in Pakistan since 1984) have the same origin.
