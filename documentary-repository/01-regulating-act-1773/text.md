# Regulating Act 1773
## An Act for Establishing Certain Regulations for the Better Management of the Affairs of the East India Company, as well in India as in Europe
### 13 George III, Chapter 63 | Royal Assent: 10 June 1773

---

## Preamble

WHEREAS the several powers and authorities granted by charters to the United Company of Merchants of England Trading to the East Indies have been found, by experience, not to have been sufficient to prevent various abuses which have prevailed in the administration of the affairs of the said United Company, as well in India as in Europe; and it is expedient that certain further regulations should be established and provided for remedying the said abuses, and for the better management of the affairs of the said Company:

---

## Part I — The Governor-General and Council of Bengal

**Section 1.** There shall be, from and after the twelfth day of October in the year one thousand seven hundred and seventy-three, a Governor-General of the Presidency of Fort William in Bengal; and the said Governor-General, together with four Councillors, shall form and constitute a Council for the said Presidency, to be called The Governor-General and Council of Fort William in Bengal.

**Section 2.** The said Governor-General and Council, or a majority of them, are hereby authorized to superintend, direct, and control all the territorial acquisitions and revenues, and all matters relating to the civil, military, and revenue affairs of the several Presidencies of Fort William in Bengal, Fort St. George (Madras), and Bombay.

**Section 3.** The first Governor-General shall be Warren Hastings, Esquire; and the first Councillors shall be Lieutenant-General John Clavering, George Monson, Esquire, Richard Barwell, Esquire, and Philip Francis, Esquire; who shall hold their offices for the space of five years, but shall be removeable before the expiration of such term by His Majesty on an address of the Court of Directors.

**Section 4.** All questions at the Council Board shall be decided by a majority of votes; and in cases of an equality of votes the Governor-General shall have a casting vote.

**Section 5.** On the death, incapacity, or absence of the Governor-General, the senior Councillor for the time being shall act as Governor-General.

---

## Part II — Subordination of Bombay and Madras

**Section 6.** The Governors and Councils of the Presidencies of Fort St. George and Bombay shall be subordinate to and under the superintendence, direction, and control of the Governor-General and Council of Fort William in Bengal, in all matters relating to war, peace, and the treatment of powers and states in India; and the said Governors and Councils shall observe and follow all such orders as they shall receive from the said Governor-General and Council of Fort William.

---

## Part III — The Supreme Court of Judicature at Calcutta

**Section 9.** His Majesty is hereby authorized and empowered, by his royal charter, to erect and establish a Supreme Court of Judicature at Fort William in Bengal, to consist of a Chief Justice and three other Judges, to be called Puisne Judges.

**Section 10.** The said Chief Justice and Puisne Judges shall be appointed by His Majesty, and shall hold their offices during His Majesty's pleasure; and shall be persons duly qualified by being barristers in England or Ireland of not less than five years' standing.

**Section 11.** The said Supreme Court shall have and exercise jurisdiction, civil, criminal, admiralty, and ecclesiastical, over His Majesty's subjects within the Presidency of Fort William, and also over all persons employed in the service of the said United Company.

**Section 13.** The said Supreme Court shall be a Court of Record, and shall have the same jurisdiction as His Majesty's Court of King's Bench has in England.

---

## Part IV — Prohibition on Presents and Private Trade

**Section 17.** No Governor-General, Governor, President, Member of Council, or any civil or military servant of the said United Company shall, either directly or indirectly, receive any present, gift, donation, or gratuity whatsoever from any of the country powers, princes, or states in India, or from any person whatsoever in India, on pain of being guilty of a high crime and misdemeanor.

**Section 18.** It shall not be lawful for any Governor-General, Governor, President, Member of Council, or any civil or military servant of the said Company to be concerned, either directly or indirectly, in any trade, commerce, or dealing whatsoever within the limits of the Company's charter, on pain of forfeiture of all such goods and merchandise so traded in, and of a penalty of five hundred pounds for each offence.

---

## Part V — Parliamentary Oversight

**Section 30.** The Court of Directors shall, once in every two months at the least, lay before the Lords of His Majesty's Most Honourable Privy Council, and before one of His Majesty's Principal Secretaries of State, copies of all letters and advices received from the several Presidencies in the East Indies, and copies of all letters, orders, and instructions sent to those Presidencies.

**Section 31.** No resolution, order, or instruction of the Court of Directors or the Court of Proprietors of the said Company, for or relating to the management of the territorial revenues of the said Company, or for or relating to civil, military, or revenue affairs, shall be sent or transmitted to India without first having been laid before and approved by the King's Privy Council.

---

## Historical Note

The Regulating Act was the first hesitant step towards Crown sovereignty over British India. It did not displace the Company — it attempted to overlay it with a structure of accountability. The dual government thus created was inherently unstable: the Council of Four frequently outvoted Warren Hastings, producing a decade of administrative paralysis. Pitt's India Act 1784 drew the lesson and created a Board of Control with paramount authority, initiating the era of 'double government' that lasted until 1858.
