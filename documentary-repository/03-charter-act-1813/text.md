# Charter Act 1813
## An Act for Continuing in the East India Company, for a Further Term, the Possession of the British Territories in India, together with Certain Exclusive Privileges
### 53 George III, Chapter 155 | Royal Assent: 21 July 1813

---

## Preamble

WHEREAS the territories in India under the government of the East India Company are held under the sovereignty of His Majesty; and it is fitting that the East India Company should continue, for a further term, to manage the civil, military, and revenue affairs of the said territories; and whereas it is expedient to make further provision for the better government of the said territories and the regulation of trade thereto and therefrom:

---

## Part I — Continuation of the Charter

**Section 1.** The exclusive privileges of the United Company of Merchants of England trading to the East Indies, so far as the same relate to the trade between the East Indies and the United Kingdom, are hereby continued to the said Company until the tenth day of April in the year one thousand eight hundred and thirty-four, subject to such regulations as shall be established by this Act.

**Section 2.** The territories in possession of the said Company in India are acknowledged to be possessions of His Majesty's Crown; and the Company holds and manages the same in trust for His Majesty, his heirs and successors.

---

## Part II — Opening of Indian Trade

**Section 5.** His Majesty's subjects may, subject to such regulations as shall from time to time be established by the Board of Control or the Court of Directors, export goods from Great Britain to India and import goods from India to Great Britain; and any goods lawfully imported may be sold and disposed of in India or in Great Britain as the owners shall think fit.

**Section 6.** No British subject shall reside or trade in India without a licence granted by the Court of Directors with the approval of the Board of Control; any person residing without such licence shall be liable to imprisonment not exceeding three months, or to be deported to Great Britain, or both.

**Section 7.** Nothing in this Act shall be construed to extend to, alter, or affect the exclusive privilege of the said Company with respect to the trade to and from China, or to and from the ports and places whence tea is brought.

---

## Part III — Education

**Section 43.** It shall be lawful for the Governor-General in Council to direct that, from and out of the revenues of the territorial possessions of the said Company, a sum of not less than one lac of rupees in each year shall be set apart and applied to the revival and promotion of literature and the encouragement of the learned natives of India, and for the introduction and promotion of a knowledge of the sciences among the inhabitants of the British territories in India.

---

## Part IV — Christian Missions

**Section 45.** Nothing in any Act relating to the East India Company shall be deemed to prohibit or prevent the residence in India of persons desirous of proceeding to India for the purpose of introducing among its inhabitants the knowledge of Christianity; provided that every such person shall obtain a licence from the Company, or, in default of a licence from the Company, from the Board of Control; and that such person shall observe all regulations established for the government of British subjects in India.

---

## Part V — Crown's Sovereignty Affirmed

**Section 101.** The territories under the government of the East India Company are held by the Company in trust for His Majesty, his heirs and successors, and are subject to the sovereignty and paramount supremacy of His Majesty; and no act done by the Company in India shall be deemed to derogate from the sovereignty and supremacy of His Majesty.

---

## Historical Note

The Charter Act 1813 reflects the shift in British commercial thought away from mercantilist monopoly and towards free-trade liberalism. Section 43 — the 'education clause' — planted the seed of the great debates between Anglicists (who argued for English-language education) and Orientalists (who argued for Indian languages), culminating in Macaulay's Minute on Education in 1835. The permission granted to missionaries had equally profound social consequences, including the 1806 Vellore Mutiny and a growing Hindu and Muslim reformist response that would eventually contribute to the independence movement.
