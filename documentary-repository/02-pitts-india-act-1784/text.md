# Pitt's India Act 1784
## An Act for the Better Regulation and Management of the Affairs of the East India Company, and of British Possessions in India
### 24 George III, Session 2, Chapter 25 | Royal Assent: 13 August 1784

---

## Preamble

WHEREAS the several provisions made by law for the regulation of the affairs of the East India Company have proved insufficient for the purposes thereby intended; and it is expedient that further and better regulations should be provided for the government of the said Company's affairs, as well in Great Britain as in India; and particularly that a certain control should be established over the civil, military, and revenue conduct of the said Company's servants in India, under the superintendence and authority of His Majesty and his ministers:

---

## Part I — The Board of Control

**Section 1.** There shall be established a Board of Commissioners for the Affairs of India, to be called the Board of Control, to be composed of His Majesty's Principal Secretary of State for the time being, the Chancellor of the Exchequer for the time being, and four Privy Councillors, to be appointed by His Majesty by commission under the royal sign manual.

**Section 2.** The said Board of Control shall have and exercise a superintendence and control over all the civil, military, and revenue affairs and concerns of the territorial possessions and revenues in the East Indies, and over the conduct of all persons in the service of the said United Company in the East Indies.

**Section 3.** All despatches and letters to be transmitted by the Court of Directors to India on political, military, and revenue affairs shall, before transmission, be laid before the Board of Control; and the Board shall have power to add to, alter, or annul the same, and to require the Court of Directors to transmit to India such orders as the Board shall from time to time think proper.

**Section 4.** In cases of emergency requiring immediate secrecy, a Secret Committee of three Directors shall be formed, who shall have power to send despatches to India without previous communication to the Board; but such despatches shall be communicated to the Board as soon as the necessity for secrecy has ceased.

**Section 5.** The Board of Control shall have access to all books, records, and papers of the Court of Directors relating to the political, military, and revenue affairs of India; and the Court of Directors shall furnish such copies and abstracts as the Board shall from time to time require.

---

## Part II — Governance in India

**Section 7.** The Governor-General and the Governors of the subordinate Presidencies in India shall henceforth be appointed by His Majesty by commission under the Great Seal, upon the nomination of the Court of Directors; and the said Governor-General and Governors shall hold office during the pleasure of His Majesty.

**Section 8.** The Governor-General in Council shall have the power of superintending, directing, and controlling all the civil, military, and revenue affairs of all the Presidencies and territories in India, and all persons employed in the service of the said Company in India.

**Section 9.** It shall not be lawful for the Governor-General in Council or the Governors in Council to declare offensive war, or to commence hostilities, or to enter into any treaty for making offensive war against any of the country powers or states in India, except for the defence of the territories held by or in trust for His Majesty; provided that nothing herein shall be construed to prevent the repulse of any immediate attack or invasion.

**Section 10.** No subsidiary alliance or treaty binding the Company to defend the territories of any Indian prince shall be concluded without the prior approval of the Board of Control.

---

## Part III — Prevention of Corruption

**Section 23.** Every person who, being in the service of the East India Company, shall in India receive any present, gift, gratuity, or reward in money or otherwise from any prince, state, or potentate, or from any person for or on behalf of any such prince, state, or potentate, over and above the salary or allowance legally assigned to such person, shall be deemed guilty of an offence against the provisions of this Act.

**Section 24.** Any person convicted of corruption under this Act shall be liable to be rendered incapable of holding any civil, military, or naval office, and shall be liable to refund any sum received contrary to the provisions hereof, with costs.

**Section 25.** Offences committed in India by persons in the service of the East India Company may be tried in England before such commission as His Majesty may appoint; and no such trial shall be defeated by reason only that the offence was committed outside the realm.

---

## Part IV — Commercial Functions Retained

**Section 32.** Nothing in this Act contained shall extend to give the Board of Control any power or jurisdiction over the Company's commercial transactions, or to deprive the Court of Directors of the superintendence and control of all the Company's patronage, civil and military, in India.

---

## Historical Note

Pitt's Act was a masterwork of British constitutional pragmatism applied to empire. It created two overlapping sovereignties: the Crown's in the things that mattered (war, peace, revenue, territory) and the Company's in the things that were lucrative (commerce, patronage). The system worked imperfectly for seventy-four years. Its fatal tension — that an army and revenue apparatus loyal to shareholders rather than the Crown was an unacceptable constitutional anomaly — was exposed by the Mutiny of 1857. The Government of India Act 1858 abolished it.
